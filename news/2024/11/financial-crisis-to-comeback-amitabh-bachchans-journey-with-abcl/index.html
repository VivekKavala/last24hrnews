Financial Crisis To Comeback: Amitabh Bachchan’s Journey With ABCL

India has a deep-rooted love for cinema and the classic Bollywood masala film can bring joy even to the most serious of viewers.

Among the legends of Indian cinema, one name stands above the rest – Amitabh Bachchan.

At 82, Amitabh Bachchan continues to be a part of consecutive hit films, winning the admiration of fans with each role.

His journey to stardom wasn’t easy.

He faced numerous challenges at the beginning of his career and even after he became a household name, he encountered significant setbacks.

Amitabh Bachchan began his acting journey in 1969 with the film Saat Hindustani but he struggled for years to establish himself in the industry.

It wasn’t until the success of Zanjeer that filmmakers took notice of his talent, leading to his rise in Bollywood.

One misstep threatened to undo his hard-earned fame.

Throughout his career, Amitabh experienced many highs and lows.

After establishing himself as an action hero with films like Mard, Amar Akbar Anthony and Sholay, he was regarded as the second superstar of Indian cinema after Rajesh Khanna.

Despite his tremendous success, not all of his films performed well at the box office and a major turning point came when he decided to venture into film production.

Seeing his popularity soar, Amitabh attempted to expand his career by entering the world of film production.

He launched his own company, Amitabh Bachchan Corporation Limited (ABCL), investing around Rs 60 crores in the venture.

Although he initially earned a profit of Rs 15 crores, the company soon faced major financial troubles.

Films produced under ABCL, such as Mrityudaata, Family, Saat Rang Ke Sapne, Aks and Major Saab, failed to attract audiences and were box-office flops.

This period of financial strain left Amitabh nearly bankrupt, as his company accumulated massive losses.

While his acting career was full of blockbuster hits and established him as Bollywood’s Shahenshah, his foray into film production with ABCL proved to be one of the most challenging chapters of his life.

In 2000, Amitabh Bachchan made a remarkable comeback with Mohabbatein, portraying the stern yet emotionally complex Narayan Shankar.

This marked the beginning of a new era in his career, where he embraced roles that suited his age but retained his signature depth and intensity.

Movies like Baghban (2003) and Paa (2009) showcased a seasoned Bachchan, while Piku (2015) and Pink (2016) highlighted his ability to charm audiences with performances that questioned societal expectations and norms.

A pivotal moment in Amitabh Bachchan’s career reinvention was his television debut with Kaun Banega Crorepati (KBC) in 2000.

More than just a game show, KBC became a nationwide phenomenon, bringing Bachchan into millions of homes.

His warmth and empathy on screen made him not just a host but a mentor, offering encouragement and advice to contestants.

KBC solidified his connection with a new generation, drawn to his wisdom and humility and established him as a beloved figure across India.
